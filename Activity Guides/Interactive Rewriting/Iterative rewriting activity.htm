Iterative rewriting

An exercise to improve UX writing in four areas

  •  

    30–60 minutes

  •  

    A paragraph or two to rewrite

  •  

    Just you, or you and a writing partner

Introduction

Words are fundamental elements of most interfaces and products (just imagine what your favourite app might look like without them!). The demands associated with UX writing — limited space, voice and tone considerations, and having different people involved over time — can work against its effectiveness.

This activity shows how to rewrite a block of text deliberately and with consideration for several important elements of strong UX writing.

Sources

This activity is adapted from similar activities described in two recent books:

  • Strategic Writing for UX (Podmajersky, 2019)
  • Writing is Designing (Metts and Welfle, 2020)

Goals

In this activity, we'll move a piece of writing through several rewrites. At each stage, we establish a goal to focus that rewriting exercise.

The four stages and goals are:

  • Make the writing purposeful. Consider what we need the text to do, and the context for that work. In this stage, don't worry too much about how many words you use to describe the key deliverable (a call to action, description of the error, etc.).
  • Make the writing concise. Remove words that aren't necessary. Keep the sentence structure simple.
  • Make the writing conversational. Writing can become terse and jarring when space (and word count) is limited. In this stage, rewrite the text to have a more conversational flow. Reading the text out loud can help smooth out any bumps. Does it feel like you're interacting with the text?
  • Make the writing clear. After several rewrites, make sure the intended message is still coming across clearly. This stage could end up being a small tweak if rewriting has gone well.

How to 

  1. Choose a piece of writing that you think could be improved. Perhaps it confuses readers, or it seems longer than it needs to be. When first learning this activity, text which is one or two paragraphs long is ideal.
  2. Learn more about the context for the writing. Where does the text live? How will readers get to it? Where might they go afterwards? How is the reader interacting with the text?
  3. Consider the purpose of the text. Why does it exist at all? What job is it being asked to do? Create a new version of the text which prioritizes this job without worrying about the word count.
  4. Edit the text for concision using the 'purposeful' rewrite from step 3. Consider the utility of each word and sentence. Think about the platform or device being used.
  5. Take the 'concise' rewrite and make it conversational by adding in some words to smooth it out. Read it out loud a few times. Will a reader know they're interacting with the words?
  6. Ensure your writing is clear by revisiting the goals for the text. Using the 'conversational' rewrite, tweak as necessary to retain the clarity you want. Consider the use of jargon, idioms, and uncommon words.

Example

Let's work through the exercise using a notification you might receive from a transit app installed on your smartphone. The city you live in provides this app free of charge. With the app, you're able to fill your transit card, view schedules, and plan your route.

You are also able to set up a profile with a credit card on file so that your transit card is topped up each month. The notification text we'll work with for this activity is shown when the credit card for the user's profile is no longer valid (often because it has expired).

Original piece of UX writing:

“The payment method you set up is no longer valid.”

This notification is quite short. It doesn't answer some of the questions a reader might have: What does this mean? Where can I fix this? What is a payment method?

Rewriting for purpose:

“Your monthly card refill will not occur unless you set up a new payment method. To set up a new payment method, open the Settings menu, then choose Payments. Your current payment method has expired.”

This rewrite certainly provides a lot more information (and is much longer!). It is good to see the impact to the user up front (the card won't be refilled automatically), and some additional detail around how to fix the situation, and what the situation actually is (an expired credit card).

Rewriting for concision:

“Your credit card has expired. Recurring payments will fail until a new payment method is added through Settings.”

This rewrite delivers on the three key points — the problem, the impact, and the recommended solution — without using too many words. But, as is often the case with such concise text, it sounds cold and almost angry.

Rewriting for conversation:

“Your monthly pass won't renew until you add a valid credit card. Visit the Profile page (in Settings) to do that now.”

This text addresses all the key elements and uses words like ‘your’ and ‘you’ to make it more personal. You can imagine someone providing support for this app to say this to the user.

Rewriting for clarity/final version:

“Your monthly pass won't renew until you add a valid credit card (the one we have on file has expired). Visit the Profile page (in Settings) to do that now."

This final rewrite is more of a tweak, adding some details around why a new payment method is required. The end result is informative, describes the situation accurately, and is easy to read.
